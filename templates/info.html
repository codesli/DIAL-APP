Understanding
Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia

A complete overview of ALL — what it is, symptoms, diagnosis, and treatment options

What is Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia?

Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) is a type of blood cancer that affects white blood cells called lymphocytes. In ALL, the bone marrow produces large numbers of immature lymphocytes (lymphoblasts) that don't function properly.

These abnormal cells crowd out healthy cells, causing a shortage of:

Red Blood Cells

Causes anemia and fatigue

White Blood Cells

Increases the risk of infections

Platelets

Leads to easy bruising and bleeding

ALL is the most common cancer in children, but it can also affect adults. "Acute" means the disease can progress quickly if left untreated.

Who is affected by this type of leukemia?

ALL most commonly affects children between 2 and 5 years old, though it can occur at any age. About 75% of cases occur in children under 6. Adult cases are rarer but require immediate medical attention.

Signs and Symptoms

Early detection is crucial. Watch for these warning signs:

General Symptoms

  • Persistent fatigue and weakness
  • Pale skin (due to anemia)
  • Frequent or severe infections
  • Fever with no clear cause
  • Easy bruising or bleeding

Other Signs

  • Bone or joint pain
  • Swollen lymph nodes
  • Loss of appetite or weight loss
  • Shortness of breath
  • Enlarged liver or spleen

How is ALL Diagnosed?

Different tests are used to confirm the diagnosis and determine the best treatment plan

Blood Tests

A complete blood count (CBC) measures the levels of different cell types. Abnormal white blood cell levels and the presence of lymphoblasts can indicate leukemia.

Bone Marrow Biopsy

A small sample of bone marrow is taken and examined under a microscope to confirm the presence of leukemia cells and the specific subtype of ALL.

Microscopic Analysis

Blood and bone marrow samples are analyzed to identify abnormal lymphoblasts. This is where AI tools can help enable faster screening.

Genetic Testing

Tests such as immunophenotyping and molecular testing help determine the specific subtype of ALL and guide treatment decisions.

The Importance of Early Diagnosis

Early detection of ALL is critical for successful treatment. When diagnosed and treated promptly, the prognosis can be significantly better — especially in children, where cure rates can exceed 85% with modern treatment protocols.

Treatment Options

Treatment for ALL is intensive but has high success rates, especially in children

Treatment Phases

1

Induction Therapy

4-6 weeks

The first phase aims to eliminate most leukemia cells and achieve remission. It involves intensive chemotherapy to clear cancerous cells from the blood and bone marrow.

2

Consolidation / Intensification

Several months

Aims to eliminate any remaining leukemia cells to prevent relapse. Uses different drug combinations than the induction phase.

3

Maintenance Therapy

2-3 years

Lower doses of chemotherapy given over a longer period to prevent cancer from returning. Patients can often return to normal activities during this phase.

Prognosis and Survival Rates

Children: About 85-90% of children with ALL achieve complete remission after initial treatment. The overall 5-year survival rate is around 90%.

Adults: Survival rates are lower but improving. About 40-50% of adults with ALL achieve long-term remission with modern treatments.

Factors influencing prognosis include age at diagnosis, initial white blood cell count, the genetic characteristics of leukemia cells, and how quickly the disease responds to treatment.
